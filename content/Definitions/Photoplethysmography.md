Photoplethysmography (PPG) is a non-invasive optical technique used to **measure changes in blood volume** in the microvascular bed of tissue. It is the technology that allows many wearable devices, like smartwatches and fitness trackers, to monitor cardiovascular health.

The name comes from the Greek words:
*   **"photo"** (light)
*   **"plethysmos"** (increase)
*   **"graphos"** (write)

### How it Works

The basic principle involves shining a light (usually from an LED) onto the skin and measuring the amount of light that is either reflected or transmitted. Here's a simple breakdown:

1.  **Light Emission:** An LED emits light (often green or infrared) onto the skin.
2.  **Absorption & Reflection:** The light penetrates the tissue and is absorbed by skin, bone, and blood. **Arterial blood is a strong light absorber**.
3.  **Light Detection:** A photodetector measures the amount of light that bounces back (reflectance PPG) or passes through the tissue (transmittance PPG).
4.  **Signal Generation:** With each heartbeat, the volume of arterial blood increases, causing more light to be absorbed and less light to reach the detector. Between heartbeats, blood volume decreases, and more light reaches the detector. These fluctuations create an electrical signal called the PPG waveform.

The PPG waveform has two main parts:
*   **AC (Alternating Current) Component:** The pulsatile part of the signal that syncs with the heartbeat and represents the change in arterial blood volume.
*   **DC (Direct Current) Component:** The baseline, non-pulsatile part of the signal, influenced by the constant light absorption of tissues and venous blood.

### Applications

PPG has a wide range of applications in both clinical and consumer settings:

**Clinical Applications:**
*   **Pulse Oximetry:** Measuring blood oxygen saturation (SpO2).
*   **Heart Rate Monitoring:** Continuous, non-invasive heart rate tracking.
*   **Blood Pressure Estimation:** When combined with other signals like an ECG, it can estimate blood pressure.
*   **Respiratory Rate Monitoring:** Detecting subtle variations in the signal caused by breathing.
*   **Vascular Assessment:** Assessing conditions like arterial stiffness.
*   **Cardiac Arrhythmia Detection:** Identifying irregular heart rhythms like atrial fibrillation.

**Wearable Devices & Consumer Health:**
*   **Fitness Tracking:** Monitoring heart rate during exercise.
*   **Sleep Tracking:** Analyzing heart rate, heart rate variability, and respiration to assess sleep quality.
*   **Stress Monitoring:** Using heart rate variability as an indicator of stress.

### Remote Photoplethysmography (rPPG)

A newer development, remote photoplethysmography (rPPG), uses a standard camera (like in a smartphone) to detect tiny, imperceptible changes in skin color caused by blood flow. This allows for contactless measurement of vital signs like heart rate and respiratory rate, which has significant potential for telemedicine.

**Advantages:**
*   Non-invasive and painless.
*   Low-cost and simple to implement.
*   Portable and ideal for wearable devices.
*   Allows for continuous monitoring.

**Disadvantages:**
*   **Susceptible to Motion:** Movement can significantly affect accuracy.
*   **Skin Tone Influence:** Variations in skin pigmentation can affect light absorption, though algorithms are improving to address this.
*   **Signal Quality:** Can be affected by sensor location, pressure, and ambient light.
*   **Indirect Measurement:** For some metrics like blood pressure, it provides an estimate rather than a direct, precise measurement.
